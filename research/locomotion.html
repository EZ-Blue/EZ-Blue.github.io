Learning-Based Control of Transtibial Assistive Devices in Physics-Informed Locomotion Simulations

Introduction

Lower extremity mobility challenges affect millions of people worldwide, with approximately 150,000 people undergoing lower extremity amputations each year in the United States alone. Among individuals over 40 with diagnosed diabetes and lower extremity disease, one-third report difficulty walking a quarter mile and climbing 10 steps without rest. Current control strategies for transtibial prostheses and exoskeletons often fall short in adapting to varied environments and user-specific needs.

Traditional control methods—such as finite-state machines and impedance control—are limited in their ability to handle the dynamic and complex nature of human locomotion. This research addresses this critical gap by exploring deep learning approaches to develop more adaptive and responsive controllers for ankle-level assistive devices.

Methods

We developed a novel hybrid learning pipeline that integrates imitation learning (IL) and deep reinforcement learning (RL) to create adaptive control policies for ankle assistive devices. This hybrid approach enables us to:

  1. Bootstrap baseline behavior through imitation learning from expert demonstrations
  2. Fine-tune with reinforcement learning to incorporate environmental adaptation
  3. Optimize control for diverse scenarios with user-compatible parameters

Our implementation leverages the Mujoco physics engine and Loco-Mujoco framework, employing a two-agent system:

  • A Variational Adversarial Imitation Learning (VAIL) agent that controls the humanoid body
  • A separate Multilayer Perceptron (MLP) agent that manages the ankle-level assistive device

 

Figure 2: Flowchart illustrating the two-agent system used for training the humanoid model with an ankle prosthesis. The VAIL agent learns from expert data to control the humanoid, while the MLP-based prosthesis agent learns to predict ankle actions based on filtered state information.

For reinforcement learning, we developed a Proximal Policy Optimization (PPO) based model through three progressive iterations:

  1. Vanilla Policy Gradient: Initial discrete-action benchmarking
  2. Basic PPO: Added gradient clipping for continuous environment stability
  3. Enhanced PPO: Incorporated Generalized Advantage Estimation (GAE), randomized batch sampling, and policy reuse

Our reward structure focused on continuous locomotion with penalties for falling:

  • +1 reward per successful step
  • -100 penalty if fallen (determined by physiological thresholds for pelvic orientation and height)

Results

Our experiments revealed several important findings about the effectiveness of different learning approaches for controlling transtibial assistive devices:

  1. Imitation Learning Performance: The MLP agent successfully replicated the kinematics and kinetics of the ankle performed by the VAIL agent under controlled conditions. Both 36-state and 22-state MLP models performed similarly, demonstrating that reducing input dimensions did not significantly impact performance.

 

Figure 3: Comparison of ankle joint kinematics (top row) between Expert agent (solid blue line) and MLP agent (dashed red line) across different conditions.

  1. Model Complexity: Simpler multilayer perceptron actor-critic models consistently outperformed more complex transformer architectures, achieving better performance with fewer parameters.
  2. Hybrid Learning Approach: Pure imitation learning was insufficient, as agents could only walk a few steps in simple environments. Pure reinforcement learning produced unpredictable results. Our best outcomes came from combining both approaches—first establishing baseline behavior through imitation learning, then fine-tuning with reinforcement learning.

Discussion

Our research demonstrates the potential of hybrid learning approaches for developing adaptable, user-compatible prosthetic controllers. The performance limitations observed with pure imitation learning highlight the importance of environmental adaptation—while imitation learning provides an efficient starting point, it cannot prepare the system for unseen situations absent from the expert dataset.

The successful combination of imitation and reinforcement learning offers a promising pathway forward. By first training a baseline model using imitation learning and then fine-tuning it with reinforcement learning, we created a system that balances expert-like behavior with adaptive capabilities.

Several key insights emerged from our work:

  1. Input Feature Space: Our results suggest that reducing the input feature space to focus on lower body components could improve model performance and efficiency.
  2. Data Balance: We found that the most generalizable ankle actuator was trained on data that balanced between perfect imitation and exploration, highlighting the importance of the exploration-exploitation tradeoff.
  3. Model Simplicity: Simpler feedforward neural networks outperformed more complex architectures like transformers for human locomotion modeling in physics-informed simulations.

Future work should focus on testing our framework with more diverse prosthetic replacements and validating performance across varied user conditions. The eventual goal is to deploy these models in real-world hardware to improve mobility and quality of life for individuals with lower extremity limitations.
